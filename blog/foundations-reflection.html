Reflections

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?
That there are still areas that I can improve upon. I have always considered myself an empathetic person but the idea of learning to better control your emotions and reactions to things - and learning about how you learn - has been really good for me.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?
First and foremost, teamwork and communication play vital roles in programming. You could be the best coder in the world but if you can't tell others how that code works, how to fix it, or explain any of the problems you come across, then you won't be much help as a coworker or teammate. The same goes for self-awareness, you need to be aware of how you are approaching learning and what you think of yourself - because if you tell yourself you can't do something, then you probably won't be able to.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?
That it has actually been useful. That sounds very prideful and maybe a little over-confident but, as mentioned before, I do think of myself as an empathetic, emotionally intelligent person. Especially when it comes to soft or human skills. But there is always room to improve and this course has shown me that. Reading about neuroplasticity and the growth mindset were the more eye-opening examples; it pointed out some weaknesses in myself that I have been aware of for a while, proved how dangerous those habits could be, and how to move forward from them.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?
Listening and looping was a little scary at first. Reaching out and hoping that somebody would pick you up, talk to you, and listen to you? I am lucky that the person I ended up practicing with was really nice and that she was willing to continue contact with the second listening and looping task. (And the pair programming.) But yeah, putting myself out there and being a little vulnerable was scary. Nobody wants to be picked last in PE.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?
When you think of a developer or an individual who codes (or ask someone to describe that person), usually the person described is not somebody with great core skills. The stereotype might have some truth to it but I think coding requires better communication than most humanities-type jobs that I have been involved in. Teamwork, communication, the ability to deal with making mistakes or being really frustrated with yourself and the problems you encounter - all of these are a part of programming and learning core skills helps make that job a little easier.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead?
I don't think so. As mentioned above, core learning is instrumental for the parts of the job that aren't coding. There is a lot more to programming and being a programmer than just coding alone. Nurses and doctors learn medicine, but bedside manner is included in that. It is kind of like that.
