Researching Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

1. Research Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the ability of the brain to “rewrite” itself or the ability to change and adapt over time by creating new neural connections. Our brains are complex organs, responsible for our mental functioning, and scientists have discovered that these complex organs can adapt to changes by physically altering their make-up. This “plastic” quality of the brain can even be used to compensate for injuries and illnesses by reassigning tasks to different parts of the brain! Cool!

Studies have shown that by simply understanding this fact – that intelligence and our brains are malleable to our own purpose – can improve performance and morale. The idea that our default intelligence is unchangeable is a grim one, so of course you’d feel more motivated knowing that’s simply not the case!

Performing a task repetitively and recalling information affects different neurons in the brain, strengthening neural connections. Basically, practice makes perfect and the more you do a task, over and over and over again, the stronger and more efficient your neural networks will become!

    Besides practicing, you can increase your neuroplasticity through numerous other methods. Such as:
  • Learning something new. Learning a new skill, language, instrument, or even a new sport helps to stimulate the new pathways in our brains!
  • Context. Putting that learning into context and recognising that learning is the formation of neural connections – how does this new skill relate to information that you have already learned? Knowing those connections of concepts helps with long-term memory retention.
  • Mindfulness. As we learned about in a previous Sprint, mediation and mindfulness are also found to promote neuroplasticity. Reducing stress and learning to control emotions helps immensely as cortisol – or stress – can reduce neuroplasticity.
  • Exercise, diet, and sleep. While all these things are shown to improve your physical health, they are also great for improving neuroplasticity! Most of all, sleep is essential for a healthy brain. While you are sleeping, your brain consolidates what it has learned over the course of the day, i.e., “memories”.


Talking about the idea of “memory” it is important to clarify what exactly we are referring to as there are, generally, two classes of memories when it comes to learned skills in psychology. The first class is known as “explicit” memories which consist of “episodic memories” for events and “semantic memories” for concepts and facts. The second class is known as “implicit” memories which would be the memory for actions. A subclass of “implicit memories” is called “procedural” memory which relates specifically to learned skills and developing habits. Examples of this being, brushing your teeth, tying your shoelaces, or riding a bike. While all memory models are important for neuroplasticity, implicit and procedural are the main target for synaptic plasticity. That idea of “practice makes perfect” falls under this procedural memory category as it strengthens those connections through repeated and persistent cellular activation.

Zerilli, John, 'Aspects of Neuroplasticity', The Adaptable Mind: What Neuroplasticity and Neural Reuse tells us about Language and Cognition (New York, 2021; online edn, Oxford Academic, 21 Jan. 2021).

2. Research Growth Mindset

A growth mindset is the belief that one’s intelligence or talents are malleable and can be developed through persistence, effort, and a focus on learning. It might take some failures, some mistakes and some struggling, but ultimately the growth mindset maintains that through perseverance and effort, the learner can succeed. But why is that relevant? Think about it like this: your mindset is your own personal sense of beliefs, and it is the way of thinking that ultimately influences your attitude and behaviour towards yourself and others. If you have a “fixed mindset” you are more likely to give up easily, lack resilience, and lack engagement in the learning environment. Which is fair, if you lack the belief in yourself that you can do something, why even bother, right?

Breaking down that belief that your current intelligence level is as high as you can go, that whatever lies beyond that is unattainable, is incredibly important for your progress as a learner.

Reading about the growth mindset in a classroom setting (I will link the reference below), really helped me to open my eyes about how I approach learning. If I am not immediately good at something, if I don’t understand something the first few times, I tend to write myself off as “dumb” or “incapable”. That has been a common factor in learning Javascript. Hearing about this growth mindset has made me realise that I need to stop putting myself down and stop settling for things that don’t deeply challenge me. For me, positive affirmation and simply believing in myself will help me to grow and achieve my goals.

Ricci, M. C. (2021). Mindsets in the classroom : building a growth mindset learning community. Routledge.
