Problem Solving

Were blocked on a simple problem?

I’m not sure whether this classifies itself as a ‘simple problem’ but recently, while working on the Javascript Kata, I was extremely stuck on the ‘deBee’ portion of the built-in methods. I was convinced that my code was correct. I spent a lot of time researching how it was supposed to look - and getting very confused by more complex coding questions.

Pseudocode is normally my favourite problem-solving technique when I am stuck, however, it didn’t seem to be helping.
// Use .split() > > // Use .filter() > > // Use .join() >> // Return
The steps were so clear and simple to me, that it was hard to figure out where I had gone wrong. I even tried explaining my thought-process out loud. Did some more research. Stared at my screen. Took a walk. Eventually, I put it to bed and tried again the next day.

It wasn’t until I looked at my code the next day that I realised exactly where I had gone wrong. And boy, was it even more frustrating. I had managed to get everything correct – even the confusing .filter() section! – but had made one tiny mistake. I forgot to put a space between my .split() and .join() ‘’s.

From this experience I learned, you should probably read the test outcome – because they normally TELL you what’s wrong. And, if a question is so confusing and frustrating that you can’t even fathom the mistake… maybe sleep on it. That isn’t always an option but coming back to it the next day with a fresh mind has always helped me, immensely.

Elegantly solved a problem.

The manipulating objects section of the Javascript Kata was definitely my favourite exercise, by far. The steps were neatly laid out, so for once I didn’t feel the need to pseudocode (I usually only do that to break up big problems). Instead, I was really forced to take each step one at a time. For me, one of my biggest issues is slowing down and not rushing or being impatient to get to the result.

I also, genuinely, tried to solve the steps by myself without researching everything beforehand. I would write what I thought was the answer then saved it and checked my answer against the test. Once I got the pattern down, the rest seemed to go by easily and I completed the first few questions without needing to research the answers online at all. (Though, I did have to look up the syntax of the delete operator and the ben property, as they were slightly out of my comfort zone.)

I also talked to myself more during this section. Which might sound odd. But muttering to myself seemed to help. Regardless, this problem felt much more calm and much less chaotic and rushed than usual. I think I have learned that I seriously just need to slow down. Stop being so impatient. Read the questions. And, most of all, have the confidence to try things on my own.

Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes:

Pseudocode.
This is my favourite problem-solving technique for breaking down big questions that seem impossible to me. I don’t use it with every single problem that I encounter but if the Javascript Kata’s have taught me anything it’s that I do well when the problem is cut up into steps. It seems much less impossible and much more manageable.

Trying something.
This is where I struggle the most. I think I have this fear of failing or not being able to do something perfectly the first time, so trying something by myself is a bit scary. Especially, if I haven’t googled how to do something beforehand.

Rubber ducky method.
It’s less talking to a rubber ducky than just muttering to myself. Sometimes with a bit of pacing included like I’m an evil genius in a sit-com. It definitely helps talking out loud; especially when I get to the parts that I haven’t exactly ironed out yet. I find that I tend to trail off and then I sit there in silence as if to say, “Okay, so what actually is the next step?”

Reading error messages.
Note to my future self: READ THE ERROR MESSAGES. Yes, they’re a little confusing to look at the first few times around. But, oh my GOSH. They help so much! When I was working on reversing a string; I kept wondering why my code wasn’t working. Turns out, by checking my error message, all I had to do was remove the space between the ‘’ (my nemesis returns!) and suddenly the letters became a single string rather than an array of letters!

Console.logging.
I’m not as confident with using console.logging. I can absolutely understand why it is important, but I tend to just run the test rather than bothering to check the console.log. I know that this problem-solving tool will be more important later on though, so I will work on this.

Googling.
Actually, I take it back that pseudocode is my favourite problem-solving tool. I love to google every. single. thing; even things that I already know for some reason? If I get stuck or can’t remember something? MDN and W3, here I come. I think this might be a developing bad habit. I am very nervous about making mistakes or not knowing things so, in a way, googling everything is a bit of a crutch for me. But I am working on that! I’m hoping that the more I learn and the more I do things, the more confident I will feel in tackling problems off the dome.

Asking your peers for help.
I will admit that I have not done this at all, so far. I’m sure it’ll be a problem-solving tool I will utilise more when we start bootcamp and actually work in teams, but as of right now… I would rather figure it out myself or ask a facilitator. Hopefully I get the chance to work on this in the future! It would be nice to be able to ask my peers and for them to ask me questions too.

Asking your coaches for help.
As mentioned above, I don’t really ask my peers for help but I am getting much better at asking our coaches. Recently, I even asked for clarification on the wording of a problem (which, turns out, I was doing wrong from the start so that was my own bad and lack of understanding). This still tends to be my last resort, though.

Improving your process with reflection. Reflection is fun! I feel like my reflection process isn’t bad at the moment, though I’m sure it could be improved. More specifically, I need to improve my review process! Going over the questions that really stumped me and researching the things that seem to always trip me up.

Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help. Consider what made you reluctant to do so. What might you try differently next time?

Recently, I asked for clarification on the wording of a problem in the Javascript Kata. I was very confused about how to approach the question. Even after I asked for clarification, I don’t think I really understood what was being asked of me. In reality, I should have pressed the issue – said I was still confused – but I felt a bit embarrassed. I mean, I’d already asked once, right?

In the end, I re-read the question and checked it over on the student handbook (which basically said the same instructions but slightly clearer). I realised it made more sense to use a for loop with a function, not a function with a for loop. But, I would have saved myself so much time and agony if I’d just pressed the issue.
